Abstract

The purpose of this study is to investigate the relationship between average teachers salary and average SAT scores per state in the US using census data from the 2012-2013 academic year. We would assume that better pay for teachers lead to greater academic success for their students. We completed a multiple linear regression model to look at the relationship between average teacherâ€™s salary and SAT scores per state, controlling for average family income, student teacher ratio, high school graduation rate, and percentage of high school students that took the SAT per state. We found that there was no statistically significant evidence that there was a relationship between average teacherâ€™s salary and average SAT score per state in the 2012-2013 academic year. Our model revealed that student SAT scores are significantly related to average family income, graduation rate, and the percentage of high school students that took the test.

Introduction

Does a teacherâ€™s salary have an effect on their studentâ€™s academic sucess? The purpose of our study was to answer this question using average SAT scores per state as a measure of success. We predicted that higher pay for teachers would cause them to invest more time into their jobs, which would translate into better grades for their students.We used the 50 states of the United States as well as the Distric of Columbia as our cases. Because we used a census, rather than a sample of states, we could not make any inferences about population.
In addition to identifying a relationship (or lack thereof) between average teacher salary per state and average SAT scores per state, we also determined if average family income, student/teacher ratio, graduation rate, and the percentage of high school students that took the SAT per state had any effect on average SAT scores. We analyzed this data using multiple linear regression, and used the p-values to determine if the variables were statistically significant in determining SAT scores using an alpha level 0.05.

Our hypotheses are as follows:
H0: There is no relationship between teacherâ€™s salary and SAT scores
HA: An increase in teacherâ€™s salary results in an increase in SAT scores

Data

We obtained data for our response variable, average SAT scores (out of 2400) of all the SAT test takers per state from the Collegeboard website. Our explanatory variables include; average teacherâ€™s salary (dollars) per state which was obtained from the website of the National Center for Education Statistics, average household income (dollars) per state was taken from the National Census Bureau,the percentage of high school students that took the SAT per state was obtained from the collegeboard website, the student teacher ratio and the graduation rate was taken from the National Education Association website. All the data was obtained was from the 2012-2013 school year. Our data was not sampled, they were taken in a census, therefore since we have data for the whole population, we donâ€™t need to make inferences e.g.Â confidence intervals, e.t.c.

Keeping all other variables constant, a $5000 increase in teacherâ€™s salary increases the average SAT of a state by 10.4 points on average. For a $5000 increase in family income, while keeping other variables constant, the average SAT score increases by 18.5 points on average. However, increasing the the student teacher ratio by 1 unit only decreases the average SAT score in a state by 0.007 points. Increasing graduaton rate by 1% only increases the average SAT score by 5.08 points, and increasing the percentage of SAT test takers by 1% decreases the average SAT score by 13.52 points on average keeping all other variables constant.

The p-values for the average teacher salary and average student teacher ratio were 0.0915 and 0.9627 respectively. This is higher than the standard alpha level 0.05 which means that we cannot reject the null hypotheses in these cases and there is no statiscially significant relationship between average teacherâ€™s salary or student teacher ratio and average SAT scores per state. On the other hand, average family income, graduation rate and the percentage of highschool students that took the SAT have p-values less than 0.05 and therefore their coeffecients are statistically significant. From the simple scatter plot we did not find any colinearity between any of the explanatory variables. We also tested for colinearity by taking out a variable at a and seeing its effects on the coeffecients in the multiple linear regression. Conclusion

Our project sought to find out whether average teachers salaries had an effect on average SAT scores per state after controlling for average family income, average teachers salary, graduation rate and percentage of highschool students that took the test. We fit a linear regression model to the data and found the coeffecient for the average teachers salary to be 2.08e-03, meaning that for every $5000 increase in teachers salary, there is a 10.4 point increase in the average SAT of a state. However the p-value associated with this coeffecient is 0.0915 which is greater than the standard alpha value of 0.05 and hence we cannot reject the null hypothesis. Hence our model did not find statistically significant evidence that there is a relationship between the average teachers salary and the average SAT score of a state. This is consistent with findings by NEA and Vanderbilt university (1,2). We had a pretty high adjusted R-squared value of 0.852, meaning that 85.2% of the variation in the date could be accounted for by our model.

The model had some weaknesses as it does not account for the cost of living which varies significantly from state to state. Other variables such as ratio of public to private schools, ethnicity, crime rate, population density e.t.c. that we have not controlled might be related to the average SAT score. Even though we find a statistically significant relationship between average income, average graduation rate and percentage of students that took test we cannot imply causation. In order for us to imply causation, we would have to conduct a controlled experiment in which we varied teacherâ€™s salaries.

After completing our study, we later found a possible discrepancy in our data. We learned that many midwestern states prefer the ACT over the SAT. Therefore, very few students take the SATâ€™s in these states, and the few that due are usually the statesâ€™ top tier students. This results in a higher average SAT score for these states in comparison to coastal states where most students take the SAT, regardless of class rankings. Since we conducted this study to determine a studentâ€™s academic sucess on average, using SAT scores as a measure of success, in the future we would also include average ACT scores in our data. We would normalize the ACT scores so they can be compared to SAT scores on the same scale to get more wholistic view on the relationship between average teacherâ€™s salary and student academic performance.
